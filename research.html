Research

Our group develops fragment-based quantum chemistry methods, molecular simulation workflows, and scientific software for studying complex chemical, biological, and materials systems. We connect theoretical method development with applications in molecular recognition, bioinorganic chemistry, binding thermodynamics, and materials modeling.

Fragment-Based Quantum Chemistry and Embedding Theory

We develop new theoretical formulations for fragment-based quantum chemistry and embedding methods. Current directions include extending effective fragment potential and effective fragment molecular orbital approaches to open-shell and multireference systems, improving QM/EFP descriptions of electronically complex environments, and developing more general mathematical formulations of EFP for robust and transferable applications.

Specific areas of interest include open-shell and multireference QM/EFP, open-shell and multireference EFMO, EFP fragments with electronically complex character, and spherical formulations of the effective fragment potential method.

Automated Workflows and Scientific Software

We build computational workflows that connect molecular structure, quantum chemistry, and binding thermodynamics. One current goal is to predict metal–peptide coordination structures directly from peptide sequences and use these structures in high-throughput calculations of metal binding free energies.

We are also interested in developing interfaces and APIs that make advanced fragmentation methods in GAMESS easier to use, automate, and integrate into broader computational chemistry workflows.

Molecular Recognition, Bioinorganic Chemistry, and Binding Free Energies

Our applications are motivated by molecular recognition problems where electronic structure, noncovalent interactions, solvation, and conformational flexibility are all important. Current projects include metal–peptide coordination and protein–ligand binding, with the goal of improving computational predictions of structures, interactions, and binding free energies.

These application areas provide demanding test cases for our methods while also addressing problems relevant to biological chemistry, environmental chemistry, and molecular design.

Emerging Directions and Collaborative Frontiers

We are interested in collaborations that connect fragment-based quantum chemistry with quantum computing, machine learning, artificial intelligence, and data-driven molecular modeling. These directions are especially promising for accelerating electronic-structure calculations, improving molecular representations, and building predictive models for complex chemical systems.

We are also exploring opportunities to apply fragmentation-based methods to polymeric systems, nanomaterials, and other heterogeneous molecular and materials platforms.
